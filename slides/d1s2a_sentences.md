Sentences and Paragraphs

# - How to improve your writing

- Reduce clutter
- Match number of subject and verb
- Use strong verbs
- Avoid passive voice
- Identify paragraph boundaries
- Use structure to clarify roles and avoid repetition

---

# - Reduce clutter – hand waving

- Avoid starting with vagueness and hand-waving
  - “Nowadays, sensors are able to gather data everywhere from businesses, infrastructures, our environment, and even ourselves”
  - “Nowadays, big data and internet of things (IoT) are prime factors of concern for any distributed system application”
  - “Every day the need for objects to be smart, with enough intelligence and sensors to perform required operations, increases.”
- In particular, watch for unsubstantiated statements about things being only “nowadays” or “increasing in interest”

---

# - Reduce clutter – clearing the throat

- Some writers feel it necessary to include a short prelude:
  - “As a matter of fact, …”
  - “In order to …”
  - “Under the circumstances …”
  - “It should be noted that …”
- These added phrases can usually be omitted without changing the meaning of the sentence

---

# - Reduce clutter – qualifiers and hyperbole

- Avoid “very”, “little”, “pretty”
  - Can usually be deleted without changing the meaning (pretty much)
- Watch for hyperbole
  - Check for ”highly”, “extremely”, “fundamentally ” and similar
- Example:
  - Frequency domain analysis of these images will generate a very significant results to understand each class very accurately contributing in improved classification results.

# - Use strong verbs

- Strong verbs make your writing lively
- Weak verbs, often paired with verbs converted to nouns, make clunky sentences
- E.g.,
  - allows monitoring monitors
  - yields a simplification simplifies to
  - use prediction predict
  - obtain estimates estimate

Based on Stanford sciwrite course

# - Avoid a lot of “to be” sentences

The main focus of this paper is on stacking containers problem in a container yard. The aim of this work is to improve the storage and retrieval operations of containers. The main resource of a container terminal is a storage area(container yard)  where the containers can be stored for a while and retrieved when a customer request takes place at the terminal. The stacking of containers will be optimised using a number of tools and techniques discrete event simulation method, fuzzy concept, and GA (Genetic Algorithm).  The containers will be stacked based on their due date and time, this will minimise the retrieval time of containers and waiting time of customers’ trucks as key performance indicators. The due dates and times of containers are uncertainty.

# - Avoid passive voice

- Passive voice has the structure “object – verb – subject” or sometimes just “object - verb”
- Active voice is “subject verb object” or sometimes just “subject verb”
- Scientists are often discouraged (wrongly!) from writing active voice
- However, active voice is more pleasant to read
- It makes it clear who the actor is
- It takes responsibility for actions

---

# - Paragraphs

- Keep paragraphs reasonably short
- Try to have 1 idea per paragraph
- Introduce main idea early (not necessarily a topic sentence)
- Context can be forgotten by the reader between paragraphs
- Lists reinforce structure and make key points stand out

# - Structure

- A strong structure helps clarify the role of each part of the writing
  - For example, a background section should only describe past work and not include any new material
- Structure helps avoid repetition
  - e.g., avoid stating the number of human subjects several times
  - Being clear that this information needs to go into a “methods” section reduces this type of repetition
  - Similarly, if you discover repetition, it could be due to lack of structure

---

# - Common scientific structuring (IMRAD)

- Many papers commonly follow a variant of:
  - Introduction; Related work; Method; Results; Conclusions
  - (sometimes known as IMRAD)
- Method and results are sometimes combined, or iterated over for different experiments
- Analysis or discussion sections might be inserted towards the end

---

# - Structure for non-scientific reports

- Non-scientific reports often need a very similar structure but with different headings
- Executive summary (often bullet pointed conclusions or main points)
- Introduction (CONVO – see next slide)
- Background (optional)
- Key idea 1 (this is like method / results)
- Key idea 2
- …
- Conclusions

---

# - CoNVO

- Context
  - What the situation is
- Need
  - What the specific problem to be addressed is
- Vision
  - What we are heading towards (and this is only one part of)
- Outcome
  - What is likely to occur if we solve this problem

From Shron M. (2014) Thinking with Data, O’Reilly.

---

# - e.g. Driver-less cars

- Context: Almost all vehicles currently require active, engaged participation of a human driver.
- Need: Human drivers are unreliable and error prone leading to loss of life and property damage.
- Vision: A passenger will key in or state a destination and need to perform no other action until the destination is reached.
- Outcome: Driver-less or autonomous cars might significantly reduce errors and the associated losses. In addition, car sharing might be easier; you won’t necessarily have to own the car. Furthermore, the passenger will be able to perform other work while being transported. Finally, traffic jams might be lessened since drives can be coordinated.
